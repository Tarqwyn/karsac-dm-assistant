      ## **Sygna**
      
      Translator and practical bridge between the party and Floki/the Greyback. Useful for language, shipboard logistics, and understated emotional interpretation.
      
      ## **Serris**
      
      Cobalt Soul scholar. Important for records, translation, symbols, and connecting fragments. Serris can accompany the party if useful, but should not solve the mystery for them.
      
      ## **Ragnfridd**
      
      Player character Skald and local cultural bridge. Chapter 3 should give him more weight. He is walking into a crisis of his own people, their truth traditions, their oral memory, and the possible corruption of witness itself.  
      Player character hooks to preserve  
      Korvann  
      The bone disc geometry connects to the corrupted mark carved into his family’s bodies. Chapter 3 should keep feeding him the sense that the thing he has hunted for ten years is not a local necromantic cult, but an imitation or corruption of something much older.
      
      ## **Xyrrathh**
      
      Xyrrathh values knowledge intensely and is disturbed by others destroying things they do not understand. The artefacts, Ashvein, the cylinder, the phial, and his whispers are all hooks. He should feel that Karsac may contain answers about what he is, but also that those answers are dangerous.
      
      ## **Rowan**
      
      Rowan responds strongly to injustice, coercion, and authority misused to protect the powerful. The Aldric/Mathr structure should resonate with him: a loyal man used by a higher power, truth buried under rank and family legitimacy.
      
      ## **Ragnfridd**
      
      Ragnfridd’s Skaldic identity should matter more in Chapter 3. The Truthspeaker tradition feels adjacent to his own but wrong in some important way. Chapter 3 can explore what witness, oath, and memory mean when official truth has been compromised.
      
      ## **Urdid / Erwing**
      
      Use depending on table attendance. Urdid can remain with the party or be temporarily backgrounded without making the story about illness. Erwing’s Cobalt message can be introduced later if not already used.  
      Chapter 3 tone  
      Chapter 3 should not feel like a dungeon immediately.  
      It should feel like:  
      leaving a city before the city decides what story to tell about you;  
      carrying evidence that may not be enough;  
      moving through a land where official structures still look legitimate;  
      discovering that the problem is not lawlessness, but law bent around an absence;  
      pursuing Mathr while realising Mathr has had sixty years to make pursuit difficult.  
      The road to Valweg should feel unsafe, but not because every mile has monsters. It is unsafe because routes, witnesses, messages, ferries, inns, and official seals may already be part of the system.  
      Good Chapter 3 opening options  
      Option A — The Greyback Departure  
      The party leave Törweg aboard the Greyback before morning fully breaks. The harbour is quiet. No one stops them. That is worse than being stopped. On board, Brynja’s folded note is opened: Mathr.  
      This gives a strong, clean transition.
      
      ## **Option B — Brynja’s Last Room**
      
      The party returns to Brynja after the dock. She gives them records, a name, and the warning. Outside, the town continues as if nothing has happened. That normality should be unsettling.
      
      ## **Option C — The Truthspeaker Consequence**
      
      The Truthspeaker has taken Vane away. Brynja explains what that means: Aldric will not face open justice. He will be thanked, retired, hidden, or removed. The party realise that official truth in Lösweg can be used to bury truth.
      
      ## **Option D — The Road North**
      
      Open with the party already beyond Törweg, on the road or water-route toward Valweg, then flash back briefly to what Brynja gave them. This keeps momentum high if the table does not need more aftermath.  
      Hard constraints for Chapter 3  
      Do not make Aldric Vane secretly evil. He is loyal, decent, and wrong.  
      Do not make Törweg openly hostile by default. The danger is quieter.  
      Do not make Mathr the immediate fight. He is the trail, not the next combat encounter.  
      Do not reveal Vishara or Maharuq fully. Let Chapter 3 deepen the pattern rather than explain the cosmology.  
      Do not make Jarl Beorn corrupted. He is deceived and may act if shown a proper case.  
      Do not let the artefact plot become a simple fetch quest. The artefacts matter because they are part of a wider binding/anchor geometry, but the characters should still only partly understand that.  
      Do not call the shared learned/trader language “Karsacian”. Karsac has multiple regional languages. The shared language exists, but it is not anyone’s first language and should not be treated as the language of the continent.  
      Do not treat the Shadow-Walkers as random assassins. They are disciplined agents connected to Yngondi, entropy, and the wider ancient threat.  
      Core Chapter 3 question  
      The clean design question for Chapter 3 is:  
      What does it take to carry an inconvenient truth from Törweg to Valweg when the person you are accusing has spent sixty years shaping the routes by which truth reaches power?  
      Important correction: language after Chapter 2  
      By the end of Chapter 2, the party’s growing magic items have reached their Named stage. This creates a collective Ashvein resonance between the four items.  
      This does not give the party full language proficiency, and it is not a universal translation spell.  
      What it does do is soften the boundary between meaning and understanding. The party can now grasp intent, sincerity, warning, emotional weight, and oath-weight across a language gap, provided at least one party member in the chain understands the language being spoken.  
      Example:  
      Floki speaks Lösweg. Ragnfridd understands Lösweg. Because of the item resonance, the rest of the party can now understand the force and meaning of what Floki is saying without needing every word translated.  
      This effect covers:  
      intent;  
      sincerity;  
      warning;  
      emotional force;  
      oath-weight;  
      the weight of verse, witness, and truth.  
      It does not cover:  
      complex logistics;  
      technical information;  
      precise names;  
      specific facts;  
      written records;  
      detailed plans;  
      specialist terminology.  
      The party still cannot simply speak every language. They still need help with documents, names, legal records, old inscriptions, and cultural context.  
      This means Chapter 3 should not be structured around “we need a translator”. Sygna, Serris, and Ragnfridd can still matter, but their role shifts.  
      Sygna is now useful as a crew/practical/cultural bridge, not as the sole route to basic conversation.  
      Serris is useful for scholarship, records, symbols, Cobalt Soul knowledge, and written analysis.  
      Ragnfridd remains deeply important because he understands Lösweg culture, oath-forms, skaldic tradition, oral memory, witness, and the social meaning behind what is being said. He is no longer merely translating words; he is interpreting weight.

# Chapter Outline: The Weight of Witness

Suggested state updates are suggestions only. Do not apply them until after play.

## Chapter Purpose

To move the party from Törweg to Valweg while demonstrating the insidious nature of a compromised truth system. The chapter will explore the difficulty of carrying inconvenient truths within a seemingly legitimate structure, highlighting the subtle pressures and dangers of pursuing a powerful, entrenched figure like Mathr. The chapter also begins to deepen the mystery surrounding the shared language and the artefacts’ resonance.

## Starting State

The party is either departing Törweg aboard the Greyback, preparing to leave Törweg after receiving information from Brynja, or already on the road towards Valweg. They possess Brynja’s records (likely incomplete), the folded note naming Mathr, and a growing sense of unease regarding the normalcy of Törweg’s response to Aldric’s disappearance. The Ashvein resonance is active, softening language barriers but not eliminating the need for skilled interpretation.

## Player Knowledge

The party knows that Aldric Vane has been taken by the Truthspeaker, implying a deliberate attempt to bury information. They have a name – Mathr – but lack concrete evidence of his involvement. They are aware that the journey to Valweg will be fraught with subtle dangers, as official structures may be complicit in concealing the truth. They are beginning to understand the potential power and limitations of the Ashvein resonance.

## Core Pressure

The pressure stems from the knowledge that pursuing Mathr will be difficult, not due to overt hostility, but due to the pervasive influence of his authority and the potential for official channels to be manipulated. The party must navigate a landscape where appearances of legitimacy can mask deeper corruption, and where speaking truth can have unforeseen consequences. The constant threat of being silenced or discredited looms large.

## Scene Spine

### Scene 1 — The Greyback's Departure (or Brynja's Last Room)
- Purpose: Establish the uneasy atmosphere of leaving Törweg and receiving the note naming Mathr.
- Location: Törweg Harbour (or Brynja’s home)
- Pressure: The unsettling normalcy of the departure; the lack of overt obstruction.
- Choices: Depart quietly, investigate the Truthspeaker’s actions, attempt to gather more information in Törweg.
- Clues: Observing subtle signs of surveillance or unusual behaviour amongst the townspeople.
- Failure: Being subtly warned off by a seemingly innocuous individual, losing the opportunity to gather more information.
- Exit State: Aboard the Greyback, heading north, with the name "Mathr" weighing heavily. (Or, departing Brynja's home, feeling watched.)

### Scene 2 — The Ferryman’s Tale
- Purpose: Introduce a seemingly harmless encounter that reveals the subtle control exerted by Mathr’s influence.
- Location: A river ferry crossing on the route to Valweg.
- Pressure: The ferryman’s evasive answers and veiled warnings about “trouble” and “respecting authority.”
- Choices: Press the ferryman for information, attempt to subtly probe his knowledge of Mathr, ignore his warnings and proceed.
- Clues: The ferryman’s discomfort and carefully chosen words, hints of local fear and obedience.
- Failure: Offending the ferryman, delaying their progress, attracting unwanted attention.
- Exit State: Continuing north, with a reinforced sense of the pervasive influence of Mathr.

### Scene 3 — The Roadside Inn
- Purpose: Provide a chance for rest and information gathering, but with the risk of being overheard or manipulated.
- Location: A small, isolated inn along the road to Valweg.
- Pressure: The innkeeper’s overly friendly demeanor and probing questions about their purpose.
- Choices: Be forthcoming with information, be guarded and evasive, attempt to gather information from other patrons.
- Clues: Overheard conversations hinting at Mathr’s power and influence, subtle signs of surveillance.
- Failure: Being subtly manipulated into revealing information, attracting unwanted attention from local authorities.
- Exit State: Leaving the inn with a sense of being watched and a lingering suspicion about the innkeeper’s motives.

### Scene 4 — The Market Inspection
- Purpose: reinforce the growing sense of Vishara's influence, and subtly hint at Mathr’s longevity while creating a tense social encounter. This encounter forces the party to confront the consequences of their actions and highlights the increasing scrutiny they are under.
- Location: Akrafjall
- Pressure: Guard Captain Brynn wants to delay the party’s access to Duvash, subtly probe their cargo for anything unusual, and assess their overall demeanor.
